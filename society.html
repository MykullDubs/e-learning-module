2. Reading Passage: The Shift from Retribution to Restoration

(A) For centuries, the dominant philosophy regarding criminal justice in many societies has been retributive. This model posits that the primary purpose of the legal system is to dispense punishment proportional to the crime committed. The underlying logic is one of moral balance; an offender disturbs the social order, and punishment restores it. However, in recent decades, criminologists and sociologists have increasingly questioned the efficacy of this approach, noting that while it satisfies a societal need for vengeance, it often fails to address the root causes of crime or aid the victims in a meaningful way.

(B) Enter the concept of Restorative Justice. Unlike the retributive model, which focuses on the law that was broken and the punishment required, restorative justice focuses on the harm caused to people and relationships. It is a process that involves all stakeholders—the offender, the victim, and the community—in a search for solutions which promote repair, reconciliation, and reassurance. The most common form of this is victim-offender mediation, where the two parties meet in a controlled environment to discuss the impact of the crime.

(C) Proponents argue that this method offers significant psychological benefits. For victims, the court process can often feel impersonal and bureaucratic, leaving them feeling marginalized in their own tragedy. Restorative justice gives them a voice, allowing them to ask questions only the offender can answer (such as "Why me?"). This can be a vital step in achieving closure. Furthermore, statistics from pilot programs in Europe suggest that victims who participate in these sessions report higher satisfaction rates with the justice system than those who go through traditional court proceedings.

(D) For the offender, the process is equally transformative. In a traditional courtroom, the accused is often encouraged by legal counsel to remain silent or deny responsibility to minimize sentencing. In contrast, restorative justice requires an admission of guilt and a confrontation with the human cost of their actions. Facing the distress of a victim can induce genuine remorse, which is a far more powerful catalyst for behavioral change than the abstract fear of a prison sentence. Consequently, several studies have linked restorative justice initiatives to a marked decrease in recidivism rates among juvenile offenders.

(E) However, the approach is not without its critics. Skeptics argue that restorative justice is "soft on crime," potentially leading to lenient outcomes for serious offenses. There are also concerns regarding power dynamics; if a victim is intimidated by an offender, a face-to-face meeting could be re-traumatizing rather than healing. Therefore, legal experts suggest that while restorative justice is highly effective for property crimes and minor assaults, it may be inappropriate for cases involving extreme violence or domestic abuse, where the safety of the victim is paramount.
