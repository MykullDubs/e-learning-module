Grammar Guide: Prepositions of Time

What are Prepositions of Time?

Prepositions of time are words that answer the question "When?". They allow us to discuss specific times, dates, durations, and periods. Understanding the difference between IN, ON, and AT is the most important skill.

1. The Time Pyramid: IN, ON, AT

A simple way to remember the main prepositions of time is to think of an inverted pyramid, moving from the most general to the most specific.

IN

General / Long Periods

Centuries, Decades, Years, Seasons, Months

ON

More Specific / Days & Dates

Days, Dates, Holidays with "Day"

AT

Very Specific / Exact Times

Times, Mealtimes, Night

PrepositionUsage
INin the 1990s, in 2025, in summer, in September, in the morning
ONon Monday, on September 8th, on my birthday, on New Year's Day
ATat 6:00 PM, at sunset, at lunchtime, at night, at Easter

2. Prepositions for Duration & Periods

For: Used to show a length of time. (He lived in Mexico for two years.)

Since: Used to show a starting point in the past. (He has lived here since 2023.)

During: Used to specify when something happens within a named period. (I fell asleep during the film.)

From... To/Until: Used to mark the beginning and end of a period. (The office is open from 9 AM to 5 PM.)

Within: Used to show that something must happen inside a period of time. (You must finish the test within one hour.)

3. Prepositions of Relationship in Time

By: Means "at or before" a specific time (a deadline). (Please submit the report by Friday.)

Until/Till: Means "up to" a specific time. (I will be on vacation until Monday.)

Before/After: Used to show chronological order. (Before the meeting, please review the agenda. We will celebrate after the project is complete.)

4. The "Zero Preposition" (No Preposition)

We do not use in, on, or at with words like last, next, this, every.

I saw her last week. (NOT: in last week)

We have a meeting next Monday. (NOT: on next Monday)

He is very busy this afternoon. (NOT: in this afternoon)
